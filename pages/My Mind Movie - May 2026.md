
THE SOVEREIGN

A Mind Movie Script

---

CHAPTER ONE: THE WAKING

The first thing I see is light.

It pours through a wall of glass to my right—floor-to-ceiling panels framed in matte black steel, looking out onto a stand of indigenous trees I planted with my own hands. The glass is perfectly clean, no smudges, no dust. The morning sun is still soft, the colour of pale honey, and it cuts long golden rectangles across the dark polished concrete floor.

The bed beneath me is low to the ground, a platform of warm teak. The sheets are white, crisp, natural linen. They are cool against my skin. I can smell the faint trace of eucalyptus from the branches I keep in a tall clay vase in the corner.

I do not reach for my phone. There is no alarm. My body woke itself because it is rested.

I swing my legs over the edge of the bed and my bare feet meet the floor. The concrete is cool, smooth, almost silken. I sit there for a moment, spine straight, hands resting on my thighs, and I breathe. The air tastes clean. It moves through the room from the open clerestory windows above, a quiet cross-breeze that carries the smell of damp earth and the distant sweetness of ripening coffee cherries from the fields beyond.

The feeling that fills my chest in this exact moment is Freedom.

Not the idea of freedom. The substance of it. It sits in my ribcage like a warm, expansive light. I am a sovereign individual. I can work from anywhere, on anything. There is no knot in my stomach about money. No tension in my jaw about what someone expects of me. No dull dread about the day ahead. There is only this: the open, quiet, radiant certainty that my life is my own.

Beside the bed, on a low shelf carved from a single block of mvule wood, sits a single object: a smooth river stone I picked up on a solo ride through the mountains. It is dark grey, cool to the touch, perfectly oval. I pick it up and turn it in my palm. It reminds me: I am solid. I am still. I am shaped by forces, but I am whole.

I rise.

---

CHAPTER TWO: THE MIRROR

The bathroom is open to the bedroom, no door, just a seamless transition. The walls are a warm, textured plaster, the colour of sun-baked clay. A skylight above the shower lets in a column of daylight. The shower itself is a single sheet of rain glass, and the floor underfoot is smooth river pebble set in resin—massaging my soles as I walk.

I approach the mirror. It is a large, frameless panel, lit from behind with a soft, warm glow. I stop and I look.

The man looking back at me is strong.

Not bulky. Not swollen. Carved. My shoulders are broad and defined, the line of my trapezius sloping cleanly into a neck that stands tall and unstrained. My chest is full, the pectorals clearly separated, the skin smooth and hairless from the cold showers I take each morning. My abdominal muscles are visible—not shredded and dry, but crisp, functional, each one sitting beneath a thin veil of healthy, hydrated skin.

The skin itself glows. It is even-toned, clear, smooth to the touch. No inflammation, no dull patches. My jawline is sharp, the bone structure prominent, framed by a beard trimmed close and precise—a line that says intention. My hair is cut short, clean, the hairline strong.

But it is my eyes that hold me.

They are bright. The whites are truly white—clear, not a hint of red or yellow. The irises are dark, deep, alert. There is no tiredness behind them. No glaze of exhaustion. No shadow of shame or hiding. They are the eyes of a man who has nothing to run from. They hold focus. They hold peace. Behind them, there is a quiet, unshakable knowing.

I lift my arm and flex. The bicep rises—full, peaked, a thick vein running along its length, visible without effort. My forearms are ropey and vascular, the hands at the end of them strong and steady. No tremor. No weakness. I turn slightly and see the broad sweep of my back in the reflection, the twin columns of muscle flanking my spine, my glutes round and firm, my legs powerful—quadriceps that sweep, calves that sit high and defined.

This is the physique of a Greek God.

And it came from me. No needle. No powder. No pill. Just discipline, nutrition, sunlight, and the natural energy that flows freely through this body because I stopped blocking it with poison and shame.

I place both hands on the cool countertop, lean in, and I speak out loud to the man in the mirror.

"You are limitless. You are sovereign. And you are just beginning."

---

CHAPTER THREE: THE MORNING RITUAL

I walk through the house, and the house responds to me.

The living area is vast and open, with a ceiling that soars to exposed wooden beams. The furniture is minimal: a low, deep sofa in charcoal linen, a single chair in tan leather, a live-edge coffee table made from a slab of ancient mahogany, its surface smooth as water. On it sits a single ceramic cup—handmade, glazed in a deep indigo blue—and a French press filled with dark, freshly ground coffee from my own fields.

I press the plunger down slowly. The aroma blooms into the room: rich, earthy, with a hint of dark chocolate. I pour. The liquid is black, steaming, with a faint crema on top. I take the cup and walk to the glass doors.

I slide them open and step onto the veranda.

The floor underfoot is warm wood decking. Ahead of me, the land rolls gently downhill—rows of coffee plants, their leaves glossy and dark green, interspersed with taller shade trees. Beyond them, a patch of deep green where the cocoa grows, the pods hanging heavy and ripening to a deep burgundy red. The air is fresh, carrying the scent of soil and vegetation and the faint perfume of jasmine growing on a trellis to my left.

I own this. All of it. Not the bank. Not a landlord. Me.

On the far side of the house, visible down a stone path, is my workshop—a separate structure with its own glass facade. Inside, I can see the warm glow of my computer screens already displaying structural models: a complex steel framework for a client's new headquarters, the stress analysis mapped in blues and reds and greens.

Beside the workshop, under a sloped, open-sided shelter with a clear polycarbonate roof that lets the light through but keeps the rain off, sit my two machines.

One is the motorcycle: a Honda CBR 500, stripped to its naked essence, painted in deep, matte black. No fairings. No bulk. Just the exposed steel frame, the sculpted tank with sharp, angular lines, and the round LED headlight like a bright, unblinking eye. The seat is firm and neatly stitched, comfortable for the long haul but slim enough to let me move. The engine sits exposed, a compact parallel-twin, clean and purposeful. It is not trying to be anything it isn't. It is honest, mechanical, efficient, and ready.

Beside it, under the same shelter, sits the car. A Subaru Impreza WRX STI, finished in World Rally Blue—a deep, vibrant pearl that shifts from electric blue in sunlight to a darker, more mysterious shade as the clouds pass. The gold BBS alloy wheels are spotless, the brake callipers behind them a bright, aggressive yellow. The hood scoop sits low and purposeful, feeding air to the intercooler. The rear wing is iconic, functional, and unapologetic.

Both machines are clean. Both are in immaculate mechanical condition. Both are mine.

---

CHAPTER FOUR: THE DRIVE — TOURING IN THE STI

Some mornings, I don't go to the workshop. I go to the STI.

I walk up to it, and the Rally Blue paint catches the early sun, glowing as if lit from within. I run my fingers along the edge of the hood—cool, smooth, perfectly waxed. The door handle is solid in my grip. I pull it open, and the door swings with that distinct, precise German-Japanese engineered weight—not heavy, but substantial. It closes behind me with a low, satisfying thud.

I sink into the driver's seat. It is a deep bucket, upholstered in black Alcantara with red stitching, and it holds my body like it was moulded for me. The bolsters hug my ribs and thighs. I adjust nothing—it is already set exactly where I need it.

The cabin is clean and purposeful. No clutter. No dangling air fresheners. No dust. The steering wheel is wrapped in leather, thick-rimmed, with the STI logo stitched in red at the centre. The gear shifter sits beside me, a polished metal sphere atop a short, mechanical stalk. The pedals below are drilled aluminium, cool and grippy.

I press the clutch. It is firm and progressive, with a clear, satisfying bite point midway through its travel. My right foot presses the brake. I push the starter button.

The engine wakes with a low, uneven rumble—a signature boxer burble that vibrates through the seat and into my spine. It is not loud. It is not obnoxious. It is a deep, contained thunder, a sound that promises power held in check by precision.

I let it idle for a moment. The revs settle into a steady, loping rhythm. I watch the needles on the instrument cluster—clean, analogue dials with crisp white markings and red needles that glow softly. The turbo boost gauge sits ready, currently at rest. The oil temperature reads optimal.

I wrap my hand around the gear shifter. The metal is cool in my palm. I push it left and forward into first gear with a precise, mechanical click. It slots in with zero resistance, no slop, no vagueness. Each gate is a defined, satisfying notch.

I release the handbrake, ease off the clutch, and feel the biting point connect. The car begins to roll. Smoothly. Effortlessly.

I pull out onto the open road beyond my land, and the STI comes alive.

The road is a ribbon of dark tarmac, curving and rising through the hills. The suspension is firm but compliant, absorbing the imperfections of the surface without jarring me. I feel connected to the road—not isolated, not numb. Every texture, every camber, every subtle shift in grip travels up through the chassis and into my hands and my seat. The steering is heavy in the best way, weighted and direct, responding to the smallest input.

I accelerate out of a corner, and the turbo spools—a quiet, building whistle, followed by a surge of torque that pins me gently into the seat. The engine note deepens, the burble hardening into a purposeful growl. I shift from second to third. The shifter moves with a slick, bolt-action precision—click-snick—and I am already back on the power, the car surging forward without a moment's hesitation.

The windows are down. The wind buffets gently, not turbulent. The air rushing in carries the scent of pine and sun-warmed earth. I can hear the gravel pinging softly in the wheel arches, the distant call of birds from the forest edge.

I am not speeding. I am not racing. I am touring. This is the pace of pleasure—fast enough to feel the engineering, slow enough to see the landscape. I glance in the rear-view mirror, and the road behind me is a clean, dark line, disappearing into the hills. Ahead, the world opens up in a panorama of green and gold.

This car is in perfect mechanical condition. No warning lights. No strange vibrations. No leaks waiting on the garage floor. The engine pulls cleanly through every gear. The brakes bite with confidence. The ride is smooth, planted, unshakeable. It is a machine that has been cared for, and it rewards me with total reliability and the kind of driving experience that cannot be bought with money alone—it must be earned through attention and love.

I drive for an hour, maybe two. There is no destination. Only the journey. The STI and I move through the country as one, and I feel a deep, quiet gratitude for the fact that I own this. Not the bank. Me. Bought outright. Insured. Fuelled. Ready whenever I am.

When I return home, I park it back under the shelter. I turn off the engine. The boxer rumble fades into silence. I sit for a moment, hands resting on the wheel, feeling the heat of the engine cooling, hearing the tick of contracting metal. I am calm. I am full.

---

CHAPTER FIVE: THE WORK AND THE WEALTH

I walk the stone path to my workshop. The door slides open without sound.

Inside, the air is cool—a quiet air conditioning hum beneath the silence. The room smells faintly of paper, coffee, and the warm electronic scent of high-end computing equipment. My desk is a single slab of black walnut, supported by hairpin steel legs. On it sit two large curved monitors, a mechanical keyboard with a satisfying weighted click, a precision mouse, and a leather-bound notebook open to a page of calculations I sketched yesterday.

I sit in my chair—an ergonomic piece in black mesh and aluminium—and I pull up the project. It is a twelve-storey mixed-use tower, and I am the lead structural designer. My firm—my name on the letterhead—was chosen because we offer something others don't: elegant, efficient, cost-effective solutions. I study the model, rotate it in three dimensions, and within minutes I see an optimisation. I adjust the bracing pattern, rerun the analysis, and watch the stress ratios drop into the green. A cleaner solution. Less steel. Same strength. The client will save hundreds of thousands.

I smile. This is creating from the divine without struggle.

A notification appears. An invoice has been paid. I glance at the number—forty-seven thousand dollars, one of several payments that arrive throughout each month from ongoing projects. I open my financial dashboard, a clean interface that aggregates everything.

The numbers are there, clear and bright.

Total net worth: $5,000,000.

Not a dream. Not a target. A current balance, distributed across investments, cash reserves, the value of this land, and the businesses I've built. The engineering consultancy. The coffee and cocoa export contracts. The trading ventures that run semi-autonomously, algorithms and trusted partners doing the heavy lifting while I sleep.

I work four to five hours a day. Focused. Creative. Deep work. And in those hours, I produce more value than I once did in eighty-hour weeks of frantic, scattered effort. Because now I am present. I am clear. I am operating from abundance, not scarcity.

Money, to me, is not a master. It is a tool. It buys me the freedom to wake up in this house, on this land, and design my day exactly as I choose. It means I can say yes to the experiences I want and no to the ones I don't. It means I can help someone in need without checking my balance first.

I close the dashboard and take a sip of my coffee. It has cooled just enough. Perfect.

CHAPTER SIX: THE PRESENCE I BRING

Later in the morning, I have a video call with a young engineer I mentor. His name is Daniel. His face appears on my screen—eager, a little nervous, holding up a set of drawings.

I lean in. I listen. Not just to his words, but to the hesitation behind them. I ask him three questions that help him see the solution himself, rather than me giving it to him. When he finds it, his eyes light up. "I see it," he says. "I actually see it."

That feeling is better than any invoice.

After the call, I walk back to the main house. A text arrives from one of my closest friends, Mark. "Ride this weekend? There's a new route up through the hills, apparently it opens onto a waterfall."

I reply: "Saturday. 6am. Coffee at my place first."

This is my circle. Small. Solid. People who have proven themselves over years. People who don't want anything from me except my company. People who tell me the truth and receive the truth from me.

There is no drama. No gossip. No hidden resentment. I have released the relationships that drained me, and transformed the ones worth keeping. My family and I are connected—calls are warm, visits are genuine, the old tensions have dissolved because I stopped showing up as the person I used to be. When they see me now, they see a man at peace, and it gives them permission to find their own.

When I walk into a room—any room—I bring calm with me. I walk with my shoulders back, my chest open, my eyes soft but direct. I speak slowly. I listen fully. People feel seen in my presence. They feel safe. They feel elevated. Not because I perform anything. Because I am not leaking energy. I am full. And from fullness, I overflow into others.

CHAPTER SEVEN: THE RIDE — THE HONDA CBR 500

It is Saturday, 6am. The sun is just cresting the hills, painting the sky in washes of pink and orange and pale turquoise.

I walk to the shelter. The Honda CBR 500 waits for me, sleek and dark and purposeful. The matte black paint on the tank is deep, non-reflective, almost absorbing the morning light. There is no fairing to hide behind—this is a naked bike, honest and exposed. The round LED headlight stares forward like a bright, intelligent eye. The steel frame is visible, the welds clean and precise. The engine, a compact parallel-twin, sits uncovered, its surfaces a mix of matte black and brushed aluminium. The exhaust headers curve down and back, clean of rust, a faint golden discolouration from heat—proof of use, not neglect.

The seat is a single, flat unit, neatly stitched and firm enough for the long rides I love. The handlebars rise to meet me in a natural, upright position—aggressive enough to feel sporty, relaxed enough to tour all day. The instrument cluster is a clean LCD, showing speed, gear, and fuel in crisp white digits against black.

I swing my leg over. The seat cushions me perfectly. The tank between my knees is narrow, letting my legs drop straight to the ground. I pull on my gloves—supple brown leather, stitched with care—and fasten my helmet, a matte black full-face with a gold-tinted visor that makes the world look warm and cinematic.

I press the ignition. The engine turns over instantly—a smooth, even idle, a quiet hum with an edge of mechanical intent. It is not loud. It does not scream. It breathes. The vibration through the tank and bars is a gentle, steady pulse.

Mark pulls up on his own bike—a dusty scrambler. He nods. We don't need words.

I click into first gear. The lever moves with a soft, clean snick. I ease out the clutch—the biting point is wide and forgiving—and the Honda pulls away with a smooth, linear surge. No jerk. No hesitation. Just forward motion, controlled and willing.

We roll out, gravel crunching under the tyres before we hit the open tarmac. The bike is light beneath me, agile, flickable. It responds to the slightest shift of my body. I lean into a gentle curve, and the bike follows as if reading my mind. The suspension absorbs the road's texture without floating or wallowing—composed, planted, comfortable.

The wind presses against my chest. The landscape unrolls around us—rolling green hills, patches of dense forest, the occasional village where the morning fires are just being lit. The CBR hums along at a relaxed cruising speed, the engine turning at a lazy, unstressed rhythm. There is power in reserve, I can feel it waiting, but I am in no hurry.

We climb. The air cools and sharpens, carrying the scent of pine and damp rock. The road tightens, and the bike comes into its own—flicking through chicanes with precision, the brakes biting smoothly when I need them, the ABS ready but never intrusive. The ride is smooth. Not harsh. Not jarring. Just connection—my body to the machine, the machine to the road, the road to the earth.

After an hour, the trees open and there it is: the waterfall. Thin and white, dropping from a high cliff into a pool of emerald green. I cut the engine and coast to a stop. The silence that follows is vast—only water, wind, and the sound of our breathing.

I remove my helmet. The spray hits my face, fine and cool.

This is pure joy. No goal. No destination that matters. Just the machine, the road, and the body that carries me.

I sit on a flat rock and look at the waterfall. The CBR rests on its kickstand behind me, engine ticking softly as it cools. I think: I own this. All of it. The bike. The day. The peace.

---

CHAPTER EIGHT: THE EVENING RETURN

The sun is low as I ride back onto my land. The sky is a deep, bruised orange. The house glows from within, warm lights turning the glass walls into panels of gold. Beside the shelter, the Subaru waits, its Rally Blue paint catching the last light, looking fast even while standing still.

I park the Honda next to the STI. Two machines. Both immaculate. Both mine. I run my hand over the matte black tank of the CBR—a gesture of gratitude—and then my fingers brush the cool blue fender of the Subaru. The engine of the bike ticks as it cools. The scent of warm metal and clean oil hangs in the air.

Inside, I shower. The hot water runs over my muscles, and I feel them release. I change into soft, loose clothes—a white cotton shirt, grey linen trousers. Barefoot, I walk to the kitchen and pour a glass of cold water infused with slices of lemon and cucumber from the garden.

I step back onto the veranda.

The sky is now a deep indigo, the first stars appearing. The air is cool, and I can hear the sound of crickets beginning their nightly chorus. In the distance, the silhouette of the hills is a dark, calm line against the fading horizon.

I sit in the tan leather chair. The same river stone is on the table beside me. I pick it up. Feel its weight. Its coolness.

I think about my life. I think about the young men and women in my community who I'll meet with tomorrow, sharing what I've learned about finding peace and meaning. I think about my family, who I'll call later. I think about the structures I'm designing—steel and concrete and glass that will stand for decades, sheltering people, doing no harm, built right. I think about the winding tarmac and the STI's gearbox clicking through the gates, the Honda's smooth hum through the hills.

I think about the fact that there is not a single area of my life that feels out of control. Not one.

My body is strong and clean.
My mind is calm and limitless.
My work is creative and abundant.
My relationships are true.
My wealth is secure.
My machines are maintained and ready.
My joy is self-generated.

I am the person who creates from the divine without struggle.

I close my eyes and I feel gratitude. Not a passing thought of thanks—a deep, cellular vibration of thankfulness that saturates every tissue. I let it wash through me, from the crown of my head down my spine, through my chest, into my belly, down my legs, and into the earth beneath my bare feet.

I open my eyes.

The stars are brighter now.

I am home. I am whole. I am free.

This is my life.

This is now.
